Section 3

Your learning journey

English is measured in levels called CEFR — from A1 beginner to C1 advanced. Tap any level to open it, then tap a skill to see what it looks like in real life.

At A1 you can introduce yourself, talk about your daily routine, and understand simple instructions. You handle basic social exchanges and familiar everyday situations with confidence.

At A2 you can handle familiar everyday situations, talk about your background and immediate needs. Shopping, making appointments, and simple conversations become manageable.

At B1 you can handle most travel situations, talk about your life experiences, and manage everyday conversations with native speakers. You can describe events and give your opinions.

At B2 you can express opinions confidently, understand complex topics, and communicate fluently with native speakers. Professional and social conversations feel natural.

At C1 you communicate with precision and spontaneity, understand nuanced language, and express ideas with sophistication. You use English flexibly for social, academic, and professional purposes.

Elsa App

Elsa is a pronunciation and speaking coach powered by AI. Weekly Community Speaking Challenge ⭐

Every week there is a speaking challenge waiting for you. Four different challenge types rotate each week — each one designed to build the specific English skills you need as a Mexican nurse preparing to work in the US.

⭐
Nunnas Recommends

Real media, real English

A song, a film clip, a podcast, a book extract — something real from American culture. You engage with it, react to it, and share your response. This is how you learn the English that actually exists in the world, not just in textbooks.

🏥
Clinical Focus

Supporting colleagues at work

Workplace communication in a US hospital setting. How to hand over a patient, how to flag a concern, how to work as part of a team when English is your second language. The conversations that happen every day on the ward.

💉
Clinical Focus

Patient communication

The language of care. How to explain a procedure clearly, how to reassure a patient, how to ask the right questions and understand the answers. The English that makes you a better nurse in any language.

🎉
General Life

Talk about your favourites

Your favourite food, your weekend, your hometown, your dreams for life in the US. Everyday conversation that builds confidence, fluency, and the ability to connect with people wherever you are.

All challenges are posted on Google Classroom each week. Your responses become part of a growing community library of real student English.

Common mistakes for Spanish speakers

V vs B

In Spanish, v and b sound the same. In English they are different.
very — top teeth touch lower lip  |  berry — lips press together

Silent letters

knife (silent k)   write (silent w)   hour (silent h)   know (silent k)

Don't add a vowel before S

Spanish speakers often say "e-special". Start straight on the S:
special   study   stress

Practice sentences — say these out loud every day

"Thank you for telling me. I think we should check your temperature."
"What is wrong? Where does it hurt?"
"The patient in room three is having trouble breathing."
"This is an emergency. Please call the doctor now."
